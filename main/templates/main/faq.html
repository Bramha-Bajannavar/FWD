Frequently Asked Questions

1. What is Parkinson's disease?

Parkinson’s disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that affects movement. It occurs when nerve cells in the brain that produce dopamine, a chemical that helps control muscle movement, begin to break down. This results in symptoms such as tremors, stiffness, slowness of movement, and balance problems.

2. What causes Parkinson’s disease?

The exact cause of Parkinson’s disease is unknown, but it is believed to result from a combination of genetic and environmental factors. A decrease in dopamine-producing neurons in the brain is the hallmark of the disease.

3. Is Parkinson's disease hereditary?

While the majority of cases are not directly inherited, there are some genetic factors that may increase the risk. A small percentage of people with Parkinson’s disease have a family history of the condition.

4. How is Parkinson’s disease diagnosed?

Diagnosis is based on medical history, a physical examination, and neurological tests. There is no specific test for Parkinson’s, but doctors often use brain imaging and blood tests to rule out other conditions.

5. How can I help a loved one with Parkinson’s disease?

Supporting a loved one with Parkinson’s disease involves patience, understanding, and helping them manage daily activities. Encourage exercise, a healthy diet, and adherence to medications. It is also important to provide emotional support and explore community resources for both the patient and caregivers.

6. How does Parkinson’s disease progress?

Parkinson's disease is progressive, meaning that symptoms typically worsen over time. However, the rate of progression varies between individuals. Some may experience slow progression, while others may see more rapid deterioration.

7. Is there any ongoing research for Parkinson’s disease?

Yes, there is ongoing research aimed at finding better treatments and a potential cure for Parkinson’s disease. Research is focused on understanding the causes of the disease, improving medications, and exploring new therapies like gene therapy, stem cells, and neuroprotective treatments.
